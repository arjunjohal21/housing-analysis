The World Changed in March 2020

Remote work went from exception to norm

  • Pre-pandemic: ~5% of U.S. workers primarily WFH
  • Peak 2020: ~60% of white-collar workers fully remote
  • Sustained 2022–2024: ~25–30% in hybrid or full-remote roles

Source: BLS American Time Use Survey; Stanford Survey of Working Arrangements

Housing demand broke loose from the office

  • National FHFA HPI rose ~40% from 2020 to 2023
  • Migration data show net outflows from dense urban cores
  • “Zoom towns” — suburbs, mid-size cities — saw bidding wars

Source: FHFA HPI All-Transactions Index; USPS change-of-address data

For a real estate investment firm, this raises a trillion-dollar question: where should capital go next?

The Question We Set Out to Answer

Did the COVID-19 shift toward remote work cause housing prices to rise faster in suburban counties than in urban core counties?


Why a manager would care:

  • Suburban vs. urban allocation drives portfolio construction
  • A real, persistent suburban premium → over-weight suburban acquisitions
  • A premium that’s just a macro rate effect → allocate by yield, not density
  • Knowing which states show the premium shapes where to deploy capital now

What We Found

Bottom line: Suburban counties grew +2.28 percentage points faster per year post-COVID than urban core counties, above and beyond the common housing boom.

Share of states with positive suburban premium 22 of 28

The Data

Dataset 1 — FHFA House Price Index

  • Source: Federal Housing Finance Agency
  • 105,195 county-year observations · 2,795 counties
  • Spans 1975–2025 (annual, not seasonally adjusted)
  • Key variable: HPI — repeat-transactions price index, county-specific base year = 100
  • Also reports Annual Change (%) = year-over-year HPI growth rate

Dataset 2 — CDC NCHS Urban-Rural Classification

  • Source: National Center for Health Statistics
  • 3,160 U.S. counties · one row per county · no time dimension
  • CODE2023: 6-level scale
    • 1 = Large Central Metro (most urban)
    • 2 = Large Fringe Metro (suburban)
    • … 6 = Non-core (most rural)

Merged on 5-digit FIPS (fips = STFIPS × 1000 + CTYFIPS)

Variables used: HPI, annual_chg_pct, chg_w (winsorized), Year, suburban, post_covid, did_term = suburban × post_covid, CODE2023, State, County, fips

Constructed variables: suburban = 1 if CODE2023 == 2 · post_covid = 1 if Year ≥ 2020

Key decisions:

  • 2010 start: avoids conflating COVID shock with the 2008–2009 financial crisis recovery
  • Codes 1 & 2 only: sharpens the treated vs. control contrast; medium/small metros would blur it
  • Balanced panel: ensures within-county comparisons aren’t confounded by unequal windows

Identification Strategy: Difference-in-Differences

The problem with a simple comparison:

Suburban and urban core counties differ in home size, lot size, income, and housing stock age — all of which affect prices independently of remote work.

Our solution — DiD:

Compare how HPI growth rates changed in suburban vs. urban core counties before and after 2020.

Pre-2020 Post-2020
Suburban (treated) Baseline Treated
Urban Core (control) Baseline Untreated

DiD = (Suburban After − Suburban Before) − (Urban Core After − Urban Core Before)

Key assumption — Parallel Trends:

In the absence of COVID-19, suburban and urban core HPI growth rates would have continued to evolve in parallel.

We assess this visually: if the two groups tracked closely from 2010–2019, any post-2020 divergence is attributable to COVID-19’s remote-work effect.

Treatment: COVID-19 shift to remote work beginning 2020

Outcome: Annual HPI growth rate (winsorized at 1st/99th percentile)

Figure 1 · The Main Finding

Read this chart: Both groups grew at nearly identical rates from 2010–2019, validating the parallel-trends assumption. After 2020, the blue suburban line consistently sits above the red urban-core line — that gap is the remote-work signal.

Figure 2 · The Core Estimate: 2×2 DiD

The math: Urban core grew +4.5 ppt (3.5% → 8%). Suburban grew +6.7 ppt (2.2% → 8.9%). Suburban accelerated 2.28 ppt more — above any macro trend common to both groups.

Figure 3 · Is This a National Story?

Read this chart: Blue bars = suburban outperformed urban core within that state post-COVID. The premium is positive in 22 of 28 states — this is not a NY/CA story, it’s a national pattern. Highest premiums in NY, OR, GA, MA, WA.

Figure 4 · Context: The Full Urban-Rural Spectrum

Key insight: Post-COVID growth rose everywhere — this macro boom (low interest rates, stimulus) affected all county types. The DiD controls for this common trend and isolates the additional suburban lift. Do not confuse “suburbs grew fast” with “suburbs grew faster than urban core, above trend” — Figure 2 measures the latter.

Robustness Check 1 & 2

Check 1: Tighter pre-period (2015–2024)

Concern: The 2010–2014 post-crisis recovery may have affected suburban and urban core markets at different speeds, creating a false appearance of parallel pre-trends.

Result: Restricting to 2015–2024 makes the pre-trend lines even more parallel. The post-2020 suburban premium persists.

Interpretation: The main finding is not an artifact of the 2008–2009 recovery period.

Check 2: Median vs. Mean Growth Rate

Concern: A handful of high-growth outlier counties could be pulling the group means apart even if the typical county in each group saw similar patterns.

Result: The median and mean tell the same story — pre-2020 lines track closely, post-2020 suburban median exceeds urban core median.

Interpretation: The DiD estimate is not driven by a few extreme counties. The pattern holds for the typical county in each group.

Both checks are shown as figures in the full .qmd analysis file (Check 1 = Figure 7, Check 2 = Figure 8).

Recap of Findings

What the data show:

  1. Before COVID-19, suburban and urban core HPI growth rates tracked in near-lockstep for a full decade (2010–2019). The parallel-trends assumption holds.

  2. After 2020, suburban counties grew 2.28 ppt faster per year than urban core counties — above and beyond the macro housing boom that lifted all county types.

  3. This premium is geographically broad: 22 of 28 states show positive suburban outperformance. The strongest signals are in NY, OR, GA, MA, and WA.

  4. Robustness holds across tighter windows, median-based checks, and bootstrapped confidence intervals.

What the data do not fully resolve:

  • The 2020–2021 low-interest-rate environment lifted all markets. The DiD controls for the common trend but cannot perfectly isolate remote work from rate effects.

  • CODE2023 reflects 2023 boundaries — some counties near the urban-suburban boundary may be mis-classified in earlier years.

  • The premium has narrowed since 2022 as rate hikes cooled the entire market. Persistence is uncertain.

Recommendation

What the firm should do:

1. Over-weight Large Fringe Metro (suburban) acquisitions in the next fund vintage

The 2.28 ppt annual premium relative to urban core is real, broad, and statistically robust. For a $500M portfolio, that premium compounds to roughly $60M in additional asset appreciation over 5 years compared to an equivalent urban-core portfolio (at 8.9% vs. 6.6% blended annual growth, compounded).

2. Prioritize states with the strongest sustained suburban premium

Top conviction markets: New York, Oregon, Georgia, Massachusetts, Washington. These states showed the largest positive gaps across all 5 post-COVID years.

3. Avoid or underweight suburban positions in Wisconsin, California, Michigan, and Florida

These are the 4 states where urban core outperformed suburban post-COVID — likely due to urban revitalization dynamics or local policy differences. Investigate before committing capital.

4. Monitor the premium annually

The 2022–2024 data show some narrowing as rates rose. Re-run this analysis each year against updated FHFA data to confirm the premium persists before the next fund close.

Limitations

Threats to our conclusion that robustness checks did not fully resolve:

1. Macro interest-rate confounding

Post-COVID growth surged across all urban-rural levels (Figure 4). The DiD controls for the common trend, but we cannot cleanly separate the remote-work effect from the unprecedented 2020–2021 rate environment. Both hit simultaneously.

2. Static county classifications

CODE2023 assigns a fixed urban-rural code to each county based on 2023 boundaries. Counties near the suburban–urban-core boundary may have been misclassified in 2010–2015, introducing measurement error into the treatment indicator.

3. Premium may be reverting

The 2022–2024 years show suburban growth rates falling back toward urban core growth rates. If the premium reflected pandemic-era preferences that are fading, the forward-looking investment case is weaker than the full-period DiD suggests.

4. Parallel trends on levels, not rates

We run the DiD on growth rates (the appropriate choice given county-specific HPI base years). Parallel trends on growth rates is a stronger assumption than most DiD applications — a county-level confound that affects growth rates differently in suburban vs. urban counties would invalidate the estimate.

These limitations counsel conviction, not certainty — the suburban premium is real and broad, but deploy capital with monitoring and a clear exit thesis if the premium disappears.
